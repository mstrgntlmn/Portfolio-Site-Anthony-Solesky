The Problemo

Alright dudes and dudettes, let’s face it. Banks can be a total bummer. There’s got to be a way to manage your money that’s simple, friendly, and available for anyone, anytime, anywhere. And is it too much to ask for low fees? It’s your money after all!

Take a chill pill! We got you.

Vapor Wallet provides the ability to make secure, encrypted transactions from anywhere in the world, and freely convert between international currencies. While digital wallets have become the trend these days, few if any provide service outside of their home country and feature tools to help promote financial intelligence. Vapor Wallet will give financial control back to the people.

All that, and a bag of chips!

  • Pay users and vendors directly and securely with data encryption and NFR technology
  • Use the budgeting tool to ensure you stay on track with spending
  • Link your current bank account for quick access to funds
  • Set up direct deposit from your employer - no bank needed!
  • Get a physical debit card that’s linked to your Vapor Wallet account for easy access to ATMs and more!

You can’t yada yada research...

In order to ensure a most excellent experience for our users, the design process began by gathering information using a variety of research methods.

The process began with a competitive analysis of several popular digital finance services, including PayPal and Cash App.

After determining the strengths and weakness of competitors, I pin-pointed areas of opportunity within the market. One such defecit was a severe lack of support outside of these apps’ home countries, as well as tools to help users learn to better manage finances.

Armed with this information, I performed a series of user interviews and surveys, focusing questions on spending, travel, and budgeting habits.

I was able to distill a basic list of features that were most wanted by my user pool:

  • A way for users to make Payments - to other users and vendors
  • A live Currency Converter, built into the Payment Tool
  • A Budgeting Tool that allows users to monitor spending - for monthly expenses, trips abroad, special events, etc. Bonus points for incentivizing users to actually use it.
  • The ability to set up Direct Deposit from an employer without an external bank account
  • Provide users with a physical Debit Card linked to their Vapor Wallet - for ATM access and in-store purchases
  • Investing tools and tips
  • A way to view Transaction History for purchases made and payments received within Vapor Wallet
  • Simple, intuitive navigation

Just go with the flow!

With a list of features in hand, it was time to map out our navigation with some user flows. Remembering that we need to keep things simple, I went with a flat menu topography for my top-level navigation. The goal was to limit the number of actions the user had to perform in order to perform all the major tasks as much as possible.

Iterative UI Design

As you may have already noticed, Vapor Wallet’s aesthetic takes us back to the 90’s.

Why the 90’s? Good question.

When I first approached the concept of designing a financial app, my initial thought was Art Deco. Heavily tied to the 1920’s, Art Deco will always have associations with banking, money, prosperity. But that’s old money. Your dad’s money. Vapor Wallet is about your money.

I wanted to do something fresh and fun with the UI.

So I jumped ahead to another more recent decade associated with excess: the 80’s.

While it was a lot of fun playing with different styles and motifs from the neon decade, and I received positive feedback on the designs, there was always something that didn’t feel right...

My first pass felt like a caricature.

While I liked some of the colors, it just didn’t feel genuine to me, like I was trying too hard.

The background was also very busy and a bit distracting.

Here I was getting closer.

As a kid, I remember flipping through the check catalogues, looking at all the different designs, and actually thinking how cool it will be when I get old enough to have a bank account and order checks of my own.

This design was inspired by those check catalogues. But again, it was just too busy, and it was still missing something. I wasn’t connecting with the design.

This one was too Tron...

I took a step back on this iteration and looked at some other financial app designs. I really did like the minimal design and the color palette here, but I still wanted to do something different for this project. Something eye-catching that wouldn’t get lost in the fold.

Enter Rocko’s Modern Life.

I was watching the recent Netflix special of the classic Nicktoon when it hit me.

My mind was teeming with pastel colors and simple, bold geometric designs.

I created my first screen concept and absolutely loved it, but I really knew I was on to something when my 12-year-old saw what I was working on and said, “Oh, that’s cool.”

High praise.

Final Thoughts

One of my biggest takeaways from this project was the ability to let go. I had to learn not to be attached to any part of my design as it could change completely from one iteration to the next based on how testing went.

I feel like my experience participating in game jams helped a lot with prototyping, as those types of events are all about creating a minimum viable product as quickly as possible, usually within 48 hours. What was a nice change from that type of design work was the ability to actually perform all of the pre-production steps that you simply don’t have time for.

I also learned what research methods help me most in my design process. For example, user interviews were fundamental in determining what features to include, but I did not find that the creation of detailed personas really contributed much to the final design.

In the end, I learned to let go of my expectations for the project and let the data drive my design. I didn’t allow myself to become attached to any piece unless I had quantifiable evidence to justify its inclusion.

I had a great time working on Vapor Wallet, and I hope you enjoyed following along with my journey.
